Expert: Gillett case in limbo after death sentence overturned, but that's not unusual

by: Lici Beveridge

date: 2018-06-18

url: https://www.hattiesburgamerican.com/story/news/crime/2018/06/19/killers-sentencing-limbo-4-years-after-death-sentence-overturned/696529002/

The Supreme Court gave a death row prisoner a second chance because one of the juror’s made racist remarks. Buzz60

(Photo: Courtesy of MDOC)

A man whose death row sentence for capital murder was overturned in June 2014 has been waiting four years for a new sentencing hearing — his case apparently forgotten and left collecting dust on a virtual shelf.

And Roger Gillett, convicted of killing two people and transporting their bodies across state lines, is still listed on the Mississippi Department of Corrections as being on death row.

While four years may seem like a long time, it's not all that unusual for a new sentencing hearing to take years to prepare, said Matthew Steffey, a professor of law at the Mississippi College School of Law in Jackson.

"For a defendant to still be going through the legal process 14 years after the date of a death penalty crime is not especially unusual," he said. "This is hardly the oldest death penalty case in America."

However, Steffey said, the longer a case is delayed the harder it is for the prosecution to present its case.

"Witnesses die or become unavailable, evidence becomes lost," he said. "There are all sorts of practical reasons to explain this away."In addition, (Patricia) Burchell was not the district attorney in the original trial, so may need more time to get up to speed on the case," Steffey said.

Gillett, who turned 44 on June 9, and his then-girlfriend, Lisa Jo Chamberlin, 45, killed Gillett's cousin Vernon Hulett, 34, and Hulett's 37-year-old girlfriend, Linda Heintzelman, because the victims would not open a safe. The two were killed in Hulett's Hattiesburg home believed to be around March 19-20, 2004.

The couple's bodies were dismembered and placed in a freezer, which was taken to an abandoned farm in Russell County, Kansas, owned by Gillett's father and where Gillett and Chamberlin were believed to be living after they left Hattiesburg.

Gillett and Chamberlin were arrested in Kansas and extradited to Hattiesburg to await trial in Forrest County.

According to Chamberlin's videotaped confession played at her trial in 2006, Hulett and Heintzelman were beaten and stabbed. Hulett died first. Chamberlin said Gillett suffocated Heintzelman with a plastic bag she gave him after they discovered Heintzelman did not die from her other wounds.

Gillett was tried in 2007. Both were convicted of two counts of capital murder and sentenced to death.

Chamberlin's convictions were vacated in March 2017 by a three-judge panel in the 5th Circuit Court of Appeals only to be reviewed six months later by the full court, which decided in March the convictions would stand.

Chamberlin is listed as an inmate at Central Mississippi Correctional Facility in Rankin County. She is the only female inmate on Mississippi's death row.

(Photo: Mississippi Department of Corrections)

Gillett's sentence was vacated on June 12, 2014, by the Mississippi Supreme Court, which upheld his convictions but on the sentence sided with Gillett, who argued the sentence must be set aside if a jury is allowed to consider inadmissible evidence that it otherwise would not have.

"The citizens of the state should be subject to the same protections when there is inadmissible evidence (allowed) to support an aggravator," said Scott Johnson with the Office of Capital Post-Conviction Counsel, in an Associated Press story on the court's decision.

That "inadmissable" evidence was Gillett's attempt to escape from the Kansas jail where he was held after his arrest. It was deemed irrelevant since it did not relate to the actual killings.

A jury must consider a number of factors when deciding whether to sentence someone to death, including the severity of the crime, for instance if it was particularly heinous or committed during another felony such as robbery or rape. Escape or attempted escape may be considered if the capital crime was committed to help the perpetrator escape, but not in Gillett's case.

In the state Supreme Court's 6-3 decision, justices said not every escape is considered a crime of violence under Kansas law. Former Justice Ann Lamar, who wrote the majority opinion, said the Kansas crime cannot be used to support a death sentence in Mississippi.

On Sept. 18, 2014, the court denied the state's motion for rehearing, again remanding the case back to the Forrest County Circuit Court.

In a sentencing hearing, evidence presented at trial must be presented again to a new jury to decided whether Gillett should receive the death penalty or life in prison without parole.

"He doesn't get a second bite at being acquitted," Steffey said.

Since theSupreme Court ruling, no other records appear on the court's website until a letter dated March 21 sent by the court to Johnson, Special Assistant Attorney General Jason Davis with the attorney general's office and Forrest-Perry CountyBurchell, asking for an update on the case's status.

Two letters were returned to the court, filed April 24: one from Johnson, the other from Special Assistant Attorney Cameron Benton.

Johnson in his letter said he is prohibited by law from practicing in any area other than capital post-conviction, so cannot be Gillett's attorney of record.

"Please accept my apology for the delay in responding to your correspondence dated March 21, 2018, regarding the above-referenced matter," he wrote. "I was under the mis-impression that a staff attorney had already answered your inquiry. When the Mississippi Supreme Court entered a decision on June 12, 2014, vacating Mr. Gillett's death sentence, it is my understanding that my representation of him was concluded.

"I say that because it appears that the District Attorney has had (since that date) the option of choosing to have Mr. Gillett sentenced to life without the possibility of parole or placing his case on the active trial docket for purposes of setting a new sentencing hearing."

Johnson was not available for comment. Multiple calls to Kelsey Rushing of the Mississippi Office of the Public Defender's Capital Defense Division were not returned.

Gillett's case, however, appears on an April 19 open docket listed on the public defender's website.

Benton's letter says he contacted Burchell about the resentencing. He said she told him she is "considering all sentencing options and has, indeed, discussed a possible plea with Gillett’s attorney."

"As of this writing, no sentencing date has been set," Benton said in the letter.

A new sentencing hearing would be involved, time consuming and costly, Steffey said. A plea could avoid a new hearing, but that would mean the death penalty would be taken off the table.

"A prosecutor is not going to brag that they've worked out a plea in a former death penalty case," he said. "It may be a resignation to the reality of the situation."

The attorney general's office said the case is in Burchell's hands. Multiple calls to her office were not returned.

The public has a right to know what’s recorded on police body cameras

by: Trey W. Chinn

date: 2018-06-08

url: https://www.hattiesburgamerican.com/story/opinion/columnists/2018/06/10/public-has-right-know-whats-recorded-police-body-cameras/681520002/

Under a proposed bill in the state Assembly, most footage from police body cameras would be kept from the public. This Milwaukee Police Department footage is from the body camera worn by Dominique Heaggan-Brown and used in his trial. Milwaukee Police Department

Images of 36-year old James Barnett of Laurel that show a bruised and battered face after he says he was attacked by two police officers are startling and heartbreaking. However, they are not unique. All across the country, newspapers and television sets have blasted these graphic pictures across the front pages and into our living rooms.

Most times, we never know the end result. Was the officer reprimanded? What did the investigation prove? Who will be held accountable? What did the body camera footage show?

In the case of Barnett, however, we know the Laurel Police Department fired two police officers accused of brutalizing him. We know that the officers wore body cameras. And we know that the Laurel Police Department has the footage but refuses to release it.

In Mississippi, body cameras are often deployed in a manner that undermines transparency, accountability, and trust. Policies of law enforcement agencies in the Pine Belt fail to provide basic privacy safeguards and bare-minimum accountability provisions.

More: Hattiesburg police 'real pleased' with body cameras

While LPD has a policy for body camera usage, it does not provide clarity on whether officers can view footage prior to submitting their report, how that footage becomes accessible by the public, or whether or not someone’s privacy rights are taken into account.

While LPD’s policy requires that body cameras are activated for all encounters, it does not provide measures for ensuring that the privacy rights of children, domestic violence victims, and people in their homes are protected. Laurel PD’s policy does provide an accountability measure, but only says that failure to follow the policy ‘could’ result in disciplinary action against an officer. Nor does it include any provision for how video can be accessed or if it will be released.

For too long, law enforcement agencies hide behind the assertion of “ongoing investigation” as an exception for releasing body camera footage. Withholding video only increases the level of discord between the community and the police. Law enforcement should not be allowed to abuse their authority in this way.

Having exclusive control over if and when footage is released or refusing to release video altogether of police brutality and police involved shootings and killings, which are disproportionally of black men, serve as an attempt to avoid transparency and accountability. The mere deployment of body cameras makes the statement that police departments believe the actions of officers are a matter of public record. Therefore, these matters are also of public concern. Policies should err on the side of public disclosure, and law enforcement agencies should release footage if consent of those involved is granted.

Dozens of law enforcement agencies are using or considering body cameras. Body cameras, while not a silver bullet to ending police misconduct, can be part of the solution, but only when adopted alongside policies that further the goals of improved policing and strike the right balance of transparency, accountability, and privacy protections.

Trey W. Chinn is the president of the Laurel-Jones County NAACP Branch.

Petal Mayor Hal Marx confirmed Thursday he plans to run for governor in 2019.

"I think the governor's office is really where I need to be as far as being able to accomplish what I would like to see accomplished for the state," he said. "I think my experience as an executive with the city lends itself to the governor's office as chief executive of the state."

The Republican has been mayor since 2009. He said the governor's office is run much like the mayor's office.

"On a municipal scale we handle a lot of the same things you handle on a statewide scale, just a larger scale, obviously," he said. "I think I could make a good representative of the state."

One of things most important to Marx is giving a voice to the voters.

"I would rather have the support of the people than the support if the elite groups that sort of control Jackson right now," he said. "The most important thing is to give a voice to the people that aren't well connected. It seems a lot of the decisions made in Jackson benefit a handful of people that are well connected and the average Mississippian gets overlooked."

For example, Marx said, the state has given millions to big corporations that haven't produced the number of jobs they promised. While not opposed to giving money to help boost the state's economy and bring in more jobs, the policies need to be looked at and used more carefully.

"I think you can get carried away and I think some of the decisions that have been made before haven't always worked out well," he said.

Marx said he has reached out to some grassroots organizations to seek their support and feels he's been well-received.

"I want to change the tone," he said. "I want to give a voice to the people who are not in Jackson. I want to make sure the average Mississippian feels their voice is heard on whatever issue may be coming up at whatever time.

"I want to represent the average person and see if can return the power to the voters instead of the special interest groups."

Petal Alderman at Large William King said he supports Marx's decision and believes the mayor would be a good candidate for governor.

"Hal's been good for the city of Petal," King said. "That's a mighty big step but I think he'll be up for the challenge. Hal comes to the table with a lot of experience."

King said Marx's academic knowledge of government is a plus as well.

"Prior to becoming mayor Hal was a government teacher — that adds to his knowledge in the way government works," King said. "I wish him the best of luck."

Before being elected mayor, he taught social studies at Petal High School from 1998-2009. Marx worked six years as a journalist for a daily newspaper in Laurel before becoming a teacher.

He believes his experience as a teacher can help lead effecting change in the state's ailing educational system.

"I want to find a solution to our education problem in Mississippi," Marx said. "We've been struggling for years and we've tried many different approaches. There's been new fads that have come along from the federal level that filters down to the state.

"I think we need to look beyond just testing and trying to follow a trend. I think we need to go back to some basics in Mississippi. We need to listen to our teachers and get input from them instead of outside consulting groups."

Petal School District was named the top district in the state by the Mississippi Department of Education.

"I think we need to approach this like the city of Petal and the school district," Marx said. "I think there's a lot of things the state can copy from how Petal operates."

The 50-year-old earned a bachelor's degree in political science from the University of Southern Mississippi in 1991 and a master's of education degree from William Carey University in 2002.

He and his wife, Mindy, have three sons, Brian, Brett, and Nathan. His parents are Harold and Barbara Marx, also of Petal.

Republican Lt. Gov. Tate Reeves and Democrat Attorney General Jim Hood also are expected to make a run for governor.

Gov. Phil Bryant is term-limited.

While the Mississippi Republican Party did not specifically talk about Marx as a candidate, Chairman Lucien Smith said he expects to see a sizable pool of candidates in next year's race.

“Mississippi Republicans have a deep bench of conservatives, and I’m confident the voters will have a slate of strong conservative candidates after next year’s primary,” he said in an email.

Mississippi women tackle football in Hattiesburg

by: Lici Beveridge

date: 2018-04-24

url: https://www.hattiesburgamerican.com/story/news/local/2018/04/25/mississippi-women-tackle-football-hattiesburg/512807002/

Members of the Mississippi Royalty Women's Full Tackle Football Team practice in Hattiesburg. The team will play its first game Saturday in Birmingham. Susan Broadbridge/Hattiesburg American

(Photo: Susan Broadbridge/Hattiesburg American)

Around two dozen Mississippi women have been working out and working hard since June to prepare for their Women's Football Alliance debut this weekend.

The Mississippi Royalty will take on the Alabama Fire in Birmingham on Saturday. Its first home game will be at 7 p.m. May 12 at Petal High School's stadium. Tickets are $7 adults, $5 children, available at the door.

WFA is a full-tackle, professional football league with more than 60 teams nationwide.

Based in Hattiesburg, Mississippi Royalty is the first Mississippi team to play in the league.

Team members come from the Hattiesburg, Jackson, Gulf Coast and McComb areas to practice each week. They say they are looking forward to their professional football debut.

"We're ready," said Regena Jackson, 29, of Jackson. "I'm looking forward to seeing how everybody does and reacts to it. I want to see who's going to hold up on their end."

"I'm looking forward to the competition, the atmosphere," said Lashonda Griffin, 34, of Ridgeland. "I'll give it all I have. I'm getting excited to get that first play out of the way,"

Jackson joined the Royalty last year when a friend encouraged her to play on the team.

"I kind of got drug along," she said. "I thought I would come to practice and try it out and if I liked it I would stay."

Like it she did, so she stayed and is now a receiver for the team.

"I like the fact that I can be aggressive," she said. "It's really not what I thought it would be. "It's a lot better than what I anticipated. Our coach is the bomb."

Three team members are Hattiesburg police officers: Tamica Hill, Lashaundra Buckhalter and Jazz Hoadley. The team's kicker is Lachan Terrell, sister of Hattiesburg High kicker Kendyl Terrell.

Tiffany Spencer, 37, of Pass Christian is an experienced player. She started searching around four years ago for contact sports to play in the area and found a team in New Orleans.

"I wanted to find a contact sport to release some stress and energy," she said. "It's fun. I always wanted to play football, by my mama never wanted me to play with the boys."

Griffin, who plays at wide receiver and cornerback, is another experienced player. She played on a team in Arkansas before joining the Royalty.

"I heard Mississippi was bringing a team to Hattiesburg and I wanted to play in my home state," she said. "The practices have been great. We have a great coach — he is very hands-on, teaching us different techniques in tackle and running.

"It's different to watch (football) on TV. It's totally different when you put your helmet and pads on."

Spencer joined the Louisiana Jazz, which folded, and later played for the New Orleans Krewe.

"I always wanted Mississippi to get a team, and now we've got one," she said.

Spencer said she is looking forward to Saturday's game.

"I think we're going to do real good," she said. "We're going to make a statement. We have a lot of speed — there's a lot of athletes on the team. We're not going to be a pushover."

Griffin traveled from Mississippi to Arkansas to play football because she really enjoyed it, but having a team in Mississippi was especially welcome.

"I first was interested in playing flag football, but I always wanted to play tackle with the boys in middle school," she said.

The team practices at San Antonio Field in Hattiesburg on Tuesday nights and Saturday mornings. Team members are responsible for their own workouts during the week.

Royalty owner Tyre Brown said she previously started a flag football team after Tracy Wolverton of Laurel invited her to coach flag football.

"I didn't have a clue about what I was doing," Brown said. "I knew I would be better on the administrative side," Brown said.

When the flag football team played another team she was intrigued by how her team's opponents played. She learned the women also played tackle football.

"I saw a difference in the way they played football," Brown said. I asked the coach about it — the ladies were not afraid of contact."

That is when Brown decided to start a WFA team in Mississippi. She hopes to create a few more teams that will become the feeders for the Royalty, which she hopes will one day become a Division 1 team.

"I want people to see that Title IX is in existence here in Mississippi," Brown said. "There are equal rights for women to be able to play. There are several women on this team who have always desired to play football, but were told 'no.' Now they have that opportunity to live that dream."

Title IX is a federal law that prohibits discrimination on the basis of sex in any federally funded education program or activity. While the WFA is a nonprofit organization that does not receive federal funds, Brown said the team will show there is equity for women in the state.

"This is also something that is going to unify our women to let them know that they can accomplish whatever they want," Brown said. "We struggle as women with outside barriers. To bring it here with women, maybe this can be an outlet for them."

One of the team's strengths is its offense, Brown said. "And we have an A-1 kicker."

"Everything is around your quarterback, I feel like we have an A-1 quarterback," she said.

Royalty players also work together to help each other and offer support.

"We're a team," Brown said. "We believe in family."

Team captain Dana Chapman of Hattiesburg said when asked to be on the team, she jumped at the opportunity.

"I love football," she said.

At first, some of the players were apprehensive about playing full tackle, Chapman said.

"Everybody was kind of skeptical about it," she said. "As far as not wanting to get hurt and how rough it was. Our main concern is we want everyone to know we're going to be as safe as we can on the field."

Chapman said she makes sure the players work out together whenever possible and that they are prepared to practice.

"We work really hard to gain good team rapport," she said. "We come together as a family on and off the field. It's been a great experience getting to know (the team members)."

The one area where the team struggles is getting everyone to practices since many players come from outside the Pine Belt.

Brown hired Anthony Dunn about two months ago to lead the coaching staff and believes his leadership has created a winning team.

"He totally turned our team around," Brown said. "His philosophy is fundamentals and safety first.

"He feels that if you are a fundamental, sound football player you will be productive. That is one thing Anthony and I share."

Dunn, who has been involved with football since he was 6, said coaching a women's team is a different experience for him.

"Ladies are different than guys, that's for sure," he said. "They grasp it faster. They ask legitimate questions — questions about learning the game."

Dunn said the women also are focused on doing their best.

"They're ambitious," he said. "There is no big reward for them playing (football) in a sense, so they are just putting everything into it with a sincere heart.

"They're not trying to showboat for anybody."

Team manager Tiffany Johnson of Bassfield makes sure the team stays on track, checking on injured players, ensuring they have proper supplies and equipment and working out issues to keep team operations running smoothly.

She also manages logistics for visiting teams and serves as the team's point of contact.

"I didn't really want to play, but wanted to be part of the team," she said.

One of the team's sponsors, Lil Jackie's Towing, has helped the team with donations, equipment and more. Other businesses that have helped the team include Izote and Purple Parrot Seafood and Steaks.

Lil Jackie's owners Angie and Jackie Terry of Oak Grove hosted a crawfish boil for the team and took them to see another WFA game so they could get a feel for how the games are played.

"This is our first year, so of course, whatever they need, we do," Angie Terry said.

She said she never liked football until she met the Royalty since her father was heavy into football in her youth.

"We never went on family trips because Dad had the football, so I despised football my whole life," Angie said. "But when it came to the women doing it, it was a whole new light," Angie said. "I'm a firm believer in 'what boys can do, girls can do better.'"

Brown said she appreciates the Terrys' support.

"They were the first ones to believe in us and give us a chance," she said. "They have just been a blessing to Mississippi Royalty."

With his administration nearly complete, Hattiesburg Mayor Toby Barker is continuing to build on his goals for the city.

While choosing who will help him lead the city, Barker also looked at how he could do it more efficiently.

"Throughout the last eight months, we’ve really been able to assess where the city is and where it can go," Barker said. "Leadership structure has been a key component of that."

Among the changes, Barker merged urban planning and federal/state programs into one department — Urban Development under the leadership of Andrew Ellard — and brought back the chief administrative officer position. Longtime city employee Ann Jones was Barker's pick for the job, bringing experience and institutional knowledge to the table.

Hattiesburg Mayor Toby Barker (Photo: Special to the American)

"(It) is a critical need in the day-to-day operations of our city, and it was important to me to bring it back," Barker said. "It’s one thing to have an idea, but it’s another to have someone you can lean on for the day-to-day things that make that idea come to life."

While some positions have changed and others were added or deleted, salaries for the city's department heads in Barker's administration are similar to former Mayor Johnny DuPree's for the 2013-17 term.

DuPree's administrators' salaries totaled around $956,000 per year. Barker's are at $878,500, which does not include a vacant public works director position, which under DuPree was paid $65,000. Once filled, Barker's administrative salaries will total $943,500 if the public works director is hired at the same salary.

Most salaries are comparable or even less than Biloxi's, which is similar in population to Hattiesburg. According to the 2016 Census estimate, the most recent available, Hattiesburg's population was 46,926, while Biloxi's was 45,975.

For example, Biloxi's chief administrative officer earns $105,000. Hattiesburg's makes $72,000. Hattiesburg's city clerk earns $65,000, while Biloxi's makes $58,532.75.

The controller (chief financial officer), urban planning director and police chief in Biloxi each make around $85,000. In Hattiesburg, the CFO makes around $65,000 (full time), the urban planning director makes $80,000, and the police chief will begin earning $95,000 in April.

Barker still has two director-level positions to fill: fire chief and public works director. But he plans to take his time finding the right people, rather than filling a position with someone who may not be a good fit.

"Finding candidates for these two positions isn’t just about finding someone who can do the jobs," he said. "It’s about buying into the vision we have for our city, being the right fit for the job and leading from the front.

"One decision I made early on was to let the leadership landscape develop on its own, and that has worked out well. We have some incredible personnel already inside city government. We brought some outside talent in to bring fresh perspective. If given the choice between a quick decision and a good decision, I'll always opt for the latter."

Not only do members of Barker's administration bring experience, they also share the mayor's philosophy of collaboration and communication. That was a must for Barker in determining who would fill the leadership roles best.

"Overall, I think you’re beginning to see more collaboration across departments and a strong increase in information sharing," he said. "In government, it’s easy to work in silos and do things because they’ve always been done that way.

"I also think you can see that our leadership team is invested in progress for every neighborhood in Hattiesburg. It’s important to me that all wards see updates to infrastructure and quality of life in an equitable way."

But city leaders are not the only ones who are helping Barker lead Hattiesburg in a forward direction.

"Every single person has a place," he said. "We encourage all employees and residents to find a niche and tap into the enthusiasm we all feel for the future success of our city."

Barker said even though his team is on a track that will continue to help Hattiesburg develop and grow, he realizes the city still has its issues and wants to address them head on.

"Turning a ship takes time, and we are very aware of our financial and organizational challenges," he said. "However, we continue to encourage our team (from the top down) to dig in and invest in the opportunity and potential of our city."

City Council President Carter Carroll said he is pleased with how Barker has been running the city in his first eight months in office.

"As far as his department head choices, they have all been excellent — he's chosen excellent people to help run the city.

"It's quite obvious when I'm out talking to people in the city. They seem to be very pleased."

Carroll said Barker has an administration where lines of communication are open in all directions.

Barker, for example, encourages council members to interact with staff, where DuPree preferred council go to him for information, Carroll said.

"Toby encourages it," Carroll said. "He calls us and lets us know things well in advance so if we have questions or problems we can discuss it and see if there is any way we can can compromise."

Carroll said Barker also speaks to each council member individually so they can talk about individual wards' needs.

"It's working well," Carroll said. "We've all known each agenda item for a while, and we've already spoken to him about them."

Ward 2 Councilwoman Deborah Delgado said time will tell the impact the new administration will have on the city.

"I've always enjoyed volunteering in the community with children."

Ball quit her accounting job in Columbia and decided to become a teacher.

"I had never realized I would like to be a teacher," she said. "I never considered that an option."

Ball had a bachelor's degree in accounting from Mississippi State, but no degree in education. That's why she decided to pursue an alternate route teacher's license. It allows candidates without an education degree to gain a teacher's license in as little as a year.

The state Board of Education recently made some changes to the alternate route license, making it easier for teaching candidates to get jobs as they pursue the certificate. And more changes may be coming. The Mississippi Department of Education has been contacting teaching officials to ask their opinion of proposed modifications that would make completing the license harder to do.

With the teacher shortage in full swing in this state, any changes to the alternate route license could have profound effects.

The alternate route teaching license is especially important in today's climate, said Jay Howell, University of Southern Mississippi assistant professor and alternate route coordinator in the Department of Curriculum, Instruction and Special Education.

"Given Mississippi’s teacher shortage, we need more than one pathway to teacher licensure," he said. "Not everyone knows they want to become a teacher at age 18 as a freshman in college.

"Some realize they want to teach after years of doing corporate or military training or after having completed a degree in a content area they love such as history, biology or mathematics. Alternate route licensure allows these people to enter into teaching, thereby expanding the pool of qualified teacher candidates for Mississippi school districts."

Candidates for alternate route certification must have a bachelor's degree with a grade-point average of 2.75 in their content area and a cumulative ACT score of 21 or a passing score on a teaching test called the Praxis. They must also pass the Praxis II in their area of certification.

Then they must complete an approved training program with two required classes in education — one in tests and measurements, the other in classroom management. Once these requirements are met, they are eligible for a three-year, non-renewable license. Then, teachers must complete two internship classes to receive a five-year renewable license.

Ben Burnett, dean of the William Carey University School of Education, said education officials from the state contacted him this summer to ask what he thought of some proposed changes to the alternate route requirements. Currently, teachers with an alternate route license cannot teach kindergarten through third grade.

Burnett said Mississippi Department of Education officials were considering adding grades 4-6 to the list of excluded classes. They were also thinking about adding a third course to the two already required.

Burnett did not want to go on record with what he thought of the proposed changes. He did say he thought the alternate route license, as it stands now, is a good thing.

"I was skeptical as a principal, early on, to hire alternate route teachers," he said. "But the teacher shortage was not a problem then.

"But then I found, in my career as a principal, alternate route teachers had already experienced another career. What they lacked in student preparation, they brought in maturity and life experience.

"I think alternate route produces good teachers. I think we would be lost in Mississippi without alternate route licensing."

Officials at the Mississippi Department of Education did not want to talk in detail about their proposed changes.

"There is nothing to share at this time," spokeswoman Patrice Guilfoyle said in an email. "Strengthening our alternative pathways to educator licensure is part of a multi-level plan.

"We are currently working to strengthen in some cases and develop in other areas a few key foundational components that are required, prior to making changes to preparation programs."

One change in alternate route licensing, to help school districts facing critical teacher shortages, has already taken place. The state Board of Education in November approved issuing a temporary one-year license to teachers who hold at least a bachelor's degree in the subject area they want to teach. A candidate could also get the license by having 21 hours of coursework at the undergraduate or graduate level in the subject matter.

“This allows a district to request to hire a candidate who has not met the requirements to get into an alternate route program," Howell said. "After that year has elapsed, (the candidate has) to go through and make progress towards enrollment in an alternate route program.

"The district can request the special, nonrenewable license be issued again in the second and third year, but only if the candidate is making satisfactory progress towards enrollment.”

According to Projection Central, the teaching industry in Mississippi will experience nearly 10 percent growth through 2024. The state Department of Employment Security projects 455 average annual job openings for elementary teachers, 350 average job openings for middle school teachers and 390 average annual job openings for high school teachers in Mississippi through 2022. It is not clear how many teachers in the state are pursuing alternate route licensing.

"School districts across Mississippi need teachers, especially in critical areas like math and science," Howell said. "The changes allow districts to hire potentially good teachers while they're still in the process of becoming certified."

For Ball, who is already teaching sixth-grade math in the Columbia School District on a non-renewable license and completing the alternate route program at William Carey, the alternate certification was the only way to go.

"It's so great," she said. "If you already have a bachelor's degree, you only have to take four classes and it certifies you to become a teacher.

"This is a job I love to come to. I'm always satisfied at the end of the day. God has led me to what I'm supposed to be doing."

Hattiesburg City Council voted unanimously Tuesday to adopt a new public records ordinance and repeal the old one.

The new ordinance is designed to be more transparent and customer-centric, said Joe Paul, Hattiesburg's customer service coordinator.

"Transparency breeds trust," Paul said in an earlier story. "Our business is the public's business, and they have a right to know."

Paul was tasked by Mayor Toby Barker with coming up with suggestions for the revised ordinance and subsequent policies and processes.

Paul met with Jackson attorney Leonard Van Slyke, an expert on the state's public records and open meeting laws, and Tom Hood, executive director of the Mississippi Ethics Commission to learn more about the state's laws and get suggestions on crafting the city's ordinance.

"It was very educational for all of us in terms of what the best practices might be," he said.

The city last updated the ordinance in 1990 and had many revisions and much of the policy was out of date, so City Council voted to throw out the old ordinance and adopt the new one.

The new ordinance says that with a few exceptions noted in the policy, "all public records are hereby declared public property and any person shall have the right to inspect, copy, mechanically reproduce or obtain a reproduction of any public record of a public body" in accordance with reasonable written procedures outlined in the new ordinance.

The ordinance also allows for lower fees charged for researching and copying requested records to further make them accessible.

"We want to be proactive in how we communicate with people," Barker said, which includes being as transparent as possible with city matters.

The nine people charged in connection to a shooting Monday at Bonhomie Apartments have ties to two gangs, Hattiesburg police spokeswoman Lt. Latosha Myers-Mitchell said Wednesday.

The shooting took place around 1 p.m. at the apartment complex at 1810 Country Club Road. More than 70 shell casings were recovered at the scene.

D'Andre Humbles, 18; D'Vonte Humbles, 18; Deidre Burns, 26; Armonte Canamore, 16; and Phillip Little, 18; are allegedly members of the Vice Lords.

Rashad Marshall, 22; Meredith Austin, 22; Willie Edna Wilson, 41; and Daija Tate, 21; are allegedly members of the Hoovers.

Both Humbles, Canamore, Austin, Little, Tate, Marshall and Wilson were charged with criminal street gang activity and conspiracy to commit aggravated assault.

In addition, Marshall and Austin also have each been charged with two counts of aggravated assault.

The Humbles brothers and Canamore also were charged with aggravated assault and shooting into an occupied dwelling.

Burns was charged with accessory before the fact.

Bond was set late Wednesday afternoon during the suspects' initial court appearances.

D'Andre Humbles, D'Vonte Humbles, Austin, Little and Marshall had their bonds set at $400,000 each. Canamore's first appearance was made through youth court. His bond was set at $320,000, court officials said.

Tate's and Wilson's bonds were set at $200,000. Burns' bond was set at $100,000.

All nine suspects remain in custody at Forrest County Jail.

Three people were injured during the shooting. Two men, ages 19 and 26, were taken to an area hospital. Both men are recovering from their injuries. The third was treated at the scene and released.
